REDEFINING ITALIAN COMMONPLACES

Commonly known differences between regions are not always reflected in the real use of the web, but other factors come in.

In order to analyze the Istat (the italian Institute of Statistics) dataset containing data on the online habits of the Italian families in 2012/2013, we started asking ourselves what we would expect as a result of the analysis and, moreover, if the results of the study would align with the overall perception of the Italians about their use of the Internet.

Frequency of internet use and activities done on the web

The first question raised was if the existing gap between regions would be reflected on how people use internet

The first question that has been raised was if the existing gap on a socio-economic level between regions, and at a national level between the North and South of Italy, would be reflected as well on the frequencies and ways Italian people use the Internet and which activities perform more often. To do so we merged the actions of the dataset into wider groups of similar activities (i.e. “Public Administration” means both downloading and uploading a form on the public administration websites).

Italian trends of web activities

Once we learned the general statistics over the digital habits for each region, the next step was to identify the trends that characterize Italy as a whole, and therefore compose an overall picture based on the percentage values of the performing of each action in the twenty regions.
The numeric values have been codified into a chromatic system, in order to give a quick comparison of the data between regions. At a first glimpse of the map we can see how the trends related to the nine groups of activities show little differences: on average the distance between the higher value and the lowest is around 10%, except for some particular areas like Online Bank Services and E-commerce where the maximum gap reaches the 25%.
The maps show small discrepancies between regions, and how inequalities are spread on the territory. Nevertheless, by better analyzing the percentage variations of the different uses of the Internet we easily notice that those trends differ depending on the kind of activity, proving the complex nature of these data which are clearly linked to a variety of socio-economic factors.

The maps show small discrepancies between regions. We easily notice that those trends differ depending on the kind of activity performed.

How internet shapes Italy

The complex nature of the trends identified can be broken down if we assume that there are different macro areas of Italy including regions whose statistical values follow similar patterns depending on the activities performed on the Internet.

Clustering allowed us to define four different areas with similar trends.

This kind of statistical grouping is called “clustering” and allowed us to define four different wider regions within Italy.

1

Among those we recognized a first one that fairly matches with the northern regions and where even if they use the web above the average they differ from all the others by holding the highest percentage of use for activities like E-commerce and Online Banking.

2

The second cluster identifies some central regions surrounding the italian capital, where the use of internet, despite not being as the most diversified, shows an overall use above the average, peaking in those activities that specifically shape our digital life, such as Communication, browsing the web for Information and accessing to Public Administration websites, we defined this group as the most generally digitalized in Italy.

3

The third cluster includes few regions in the Mid-South of Italy where all the activities follow on average the national trend, with neither peaks or troughs.

4The fourth statistical group inside Italy merges the most southern regions which are particular for holding the primate of using the internet primarily for Leisure and entertainment purposes and Social Network websites.

What still makes the difference is how Italians deal with money online

Overlaying clusters

After dividing the regions in four different clusters and analyzing them one by one, it is interesting to overlap them, in order to compare the different trends of the activities done on the web.
The values obtained from this comparison are quite similar, with gradual variations concerning Leisure, Job and Information.
Concerning the activities related to Communication and Education, we can notice that tendencies change slightly from cluster to cluster, but their differences are wider than the activities mentioned before. The first cluster has the higher peak for Communication and the lower one for Education. For E-commerce and Public Administration the clusters are divided mainly in two values (Cluster 1/2 and Cluster 3/4).
Concerning the use of Social Networks, the only different cluster is the first one, with a lower value if compared with the others.
Regarding Online Bank Services the first two clusters have very different values,while the third and the fourth have similar trends.
The two bargraphs show a deeper insight of the two activities with more different trends: Online Bank Servives and E-commerce.

The influence of education over the web abilities

To better explore the relationship between regions and Internet usage it’s been taken into account another factor: the educational level of the individuals who underwent the questionnaire, whether they completed a university degree, either high school, middle school, elementary school or held no title at all (those last two have been merged since their statistical behaviour regarding the internet habits didn’t show any significant differences).
The graph is divided according to the number of actions analyzed previously, calculated in percentage region by region; each number of activities has been divided according to the percentage of people with a certain educational level.
This interactive visualization allows the user to overlay the region bars with the data about the educational level of Italian citizens, therefore it’s possible to see how education is related to how much and for what we use the Internet.
For example, by clicking here, we can see that people with middle school and elementary degree share the same behaviour when considering zero, one or two activities.
In another analysis, by activating middle or high school degrees, we can notice that the thicknesses of both bars show a similar trend in the range from three to five activities.
At last, those with high school and university degree perform similarly for eight or nine activities. As we can see, by selecting the degrees one by one, the habits of lower and higher degrees are inversely proportional.

Those who are educated the most generally own more skills.

If geography levels the dissimilarities, what really makes a difference is education.

Our starting point was the idea of finding the differences of internet use spread on the geographical areas of Italy. As we went on analyzing the data we had, we found out that what actually makes the main differences is the level of education people own.
Indeed, in those graphics in which we compared the values from region to region, small differences can be noticed, while the last step of our analysis shows these values complemented with the study titles.
We hence can draw the conclusion that people with a higher level of education on average own more skills than people with less qualifications.
