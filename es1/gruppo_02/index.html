Digitalia

Documenting the digital literacy void in the Bel Paese

“Becoming literate in how the technical world works is equivalent to reading, writing and maths. We need to look at this fourth literacy as mainstream.” (Paul Gilster, 1997)

Half of the Italian population never used the Internet

> This is the shocking evidence that got us started in our research: almost half of our fellow citizens never used the Internet and to better understand this alarming fact we sorted the population in four different categories, distinguish from frequency of Internet use and informatics skills (according to the Digicomp Framework, an official document written by the European Parliament).

The North-South divide

> We questioned ourselves about the relation between digital literacy and geographic location: are people actually more literate depending on the area in Italy they live in? We considered each region and we compared the percentage of population belonging to each digital literacy level. What we noticed is a concentration of digitally literates in the North(2) and digitally illiterates in the South(1).

No country for old men (neither for young ones!)

> Comparing the digital literacy level related to people’s different range of age, we weren’t surprised to see that 15 to 50 year-old people are the most digitally literate. As expected, individuals younger than age 15 and older than age 50 tend to be thoroughly digitally illiterate (1).

> In the following steps, as thought, we noticed a correspondence between what we might define “traditional” literacy and digital one: a higher level of education corresponds to a higher level of digital literacy (2).

A European overview

> What our analyse highlights and confirms is a huge gap in the Italian population. We can’t tell what the causes of the digital literacy void in Italy are, but what we are sure about is that Italy still doesn’t fit in a digital European scenery, appearing to be the third to last country in the Digital Literate European Countries Classification.
